Engineering a data lake for digital acquisitions at Chase

During my tenure at JPMC, I spearheaded a transformative technical project centered around the construction of a comprehensive data lake using behavioral clickstream data related to the acquisition process. My role involved delving deep into the user journey, meticulously tracing and recording all links and pages that led to customer applications. Through reverse-engineering this intricate path, I developed a highly efficient Teradata script that skillfully organized this vast dataset into a structured table.

This data table represented a significant breakthrough, offering us the capability to filter and analyze critical fields such as product types, customer demographics, link types (distinguishing advertisements from static links), and more. For the first time, we gained the capacity to attribute acquisition volumes to specific links and pages, thereby unearthing invaluable insights into our digital acquisition strategies. Furthermore, it provided a holistic view of acquisition volumes and the revenue generated from these efforts.

The impact of this project was nothing short of profound. It empowered the consumer bank with the ability to conduct data-driven tests to assess the effectiveness of advertisements for different products and ascertain which links resonated most with specific customer segments. Additionally, it facilitated the optimization of user experience elements to maximize performance metrics. In essence, this initiative not only elevated our data analytics capabilities but also empowered us to make more informed decisions and fine-tune our digital acquisition strategies, ultimately playing a pivotal role in contributing to the bank's overall success.

Fixing Chase's platform-wide Announcement feature

In my role at JP Morgan Chase, I played a pivotal part in identifying and addressing a critical issue related to the effectiveness of the "Announcements" feature. It all began when I embarked on fundamental research to assess the feature's performance. What caught my attention was that the Announcements were consistently underperforming, falling far below the expected metrics. It became evident that a significantly lower number of users were actually seeing these announcements, even though the feature was designed to reach a broader audience, specifically those who logged in frequently. In some instances, the visibility rate was as low as 30%.

Upon digging deeper, I uncovered several key insights that shed light on the problem. First, I realized that all "Sign in interstitials," including "Announcements," were competing for priority in terms of display. Second, I discovered that the platform responsible for showcasing these interstitials wasn't under the jurisdiction of any internal team, resulting in a lack of controls and design guidelines. Third, a closer examination of other "Sign in interstitials" revealed a stark lack of design cohesion across the board. Lastly, I found that some of these interstitials were broken, repeatedly disrupting users instead of being dismissed after the initial display. User feedback in our Voice of Customer (VOC) channels confirmed that this issue was causing significant frustration.

To tackle this issue head-on, my team within the organization took charge of the "Sign in interstitials" platform. We implemented comprehensive design guidelines, ensuring consistency and effectiveness across all interstitials. Additionally, we fixed the broken ones, ensuring that they would function correctly and no longer disrupt users excessively. This initiative not only improved the user experience but also enhanced our ability to effectively communicate important announcements to our customers.
